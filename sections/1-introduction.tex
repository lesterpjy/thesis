\chapter{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Many experiments are defined not only by \emph{what} is done but by \emph{when} it is
done. In a biochemical reaction network, adding the same reagent early or late can lead
to different products; in a bioreactor, the same total feed can give a high or a low
yield depending on its timing; in an epidemic, the same intervention has different value
early or late in an outbreak. The same pattern appears across wastewater treatment,
cell-free biosynthesis, dynamic metabolism, bioprocess control, and battery
cycling, where the outcome depends on the schedule of inputs and not only on their
totals~\citep{henze2000activated,silverman2020cellfree,martin2023dynamic,espinelrios2024cybergenetics,vanvlijmen2025battery}.
Recent experimental work makes the point concrete. Timed additions to an enzymatic
reaction cascade raise yields well above conventional all-at-once
dosing~\citep{jakstaite2026timedbatch}, and actively designed perturbation sequences can
steer intricate reaction networks that fixed protocols cannot~\citep{vansluijs2024iterative}.
In these settings an experiment is not a single condition but a schedule: a sequence of
interventions and measurements chosen over time.

Choosing such a schedule well is hard for two reasons. The experiments are expensive, in
time, material, or effort, so only a few can be run. And the space of schedules is large,
because at each step the experimenter may choose both the size and the timing of the next
action in light of the observed history so far. Selecting inputs one step at a time, using the
outcomes to decide the next move, turns experimental design into a sequential
control problem over a growing, history-dependent space. Intuition and fixed protocols
are weak tools here, and exhaustive search is impossible, so the choice has to be made by
an algorithm that reasons about what each candidate experiment is expected to learn.

Sequential Bayesian experimental design (BED) is the established framework for making
that choice~\citep{lindley1956measure,chaloner1995bayesian,ryan2016review,rainforth2024modern}.
It places a prior over the unknown parameters of the system and, at each step, selects
the experiment expected to be most informative, where informativeness is measured by the
expected information gain: the expected reduction in posterior uncertainty about the
parameters after the experiment is run. Running this rule forward produces an adaptive
sequence of experiments, each chosen in light of the last, which is exactly the regime
the timed-input problems above require.


Applying this framework to real dynamical systems is computationally demanding. The expected information gain depends on the marginal
likelihood of the data, which averages the simulator over the prior and has no closed form,
so it must be estimated by nested Monte Carlo: an outer average over possible outcomes and an
inner average that runs the simulator many times to approximate the
marginal~\citep{ryan2016review,rainforth2018nesting,huan2014simulation}. Scoring one candidate
design therefore costs many simulator runs, and the methods that make sequential design
practical reduce this cost in one of two ways: by amortising the computation into a network
trained once offline, or by optimising designs with gradients taken through the simulator.
Both shortcuts assume the simulator is cheap to run forward and well behaved to differentiate.
The systems in which timing matters most violate both assumptions, through three properties
that compound.

The first property is stiffness, the feature of chemical-kinetic systems for which the term was
originally coined~\citep{curtiss1952integration}. A system of differential equations is stiff
when its solution combines processes on widely separated timescales. Biochemical networks are
stiff almost by default, because their rate constants span several orders of magnitude, with
fast reversible binding alongside slow catalysis and slower degradation; in practice most ODE
models in computational biology are stiff~\citep{stadter2021benchmarking}. Stiffness carries
a numerical penalty. Explicit solvers must take extremely small steps to stay numerically
stable even where the trajectory is smooth, so stiff systems are integrated instead with
implicit solvers that solve a nonlinear system at every step~\citep{hairer1996solving}, at a
cost well above a single forward evaluation. The penalty is heavier when gradients are needed.
Differentiating through an implicit solver by the adjoint method~\citep{chen2018neural} adds a
large compilation overhead, is numerically delicate, and is prone to vanishing or exploding
gradients on stiff dynamics, difficulties that have driven a dedicated line of work on
differentiable and reversible stiff
solvers~\citep{kim2024stiff,pal2023neural,kidger2024efficient,caldana2025neural,linot2025vanishing}.
For experimental design this penalty is paid twice over: the inner Monte Carlo loop is slow
because each of its many likelihood evaluations is an implicit solve, and any gradient-based
design optimisation or policy training is fragile because those gradients pass through the same
solver.

The second and third properties concern the parameters. Kinetic parameters in biochemical
models are typically known only to within several orders of magnitude, and many remain only
weakly constrained even by careful experiments, a pervasive feature of systems-biology models
often described as parameter sloppiness or practical
non-identifiability~\citep{gutenkunst2007universally,raue2009identifiability,villaverde2016structural}.
The prior is therefore broad. At the same time, because stiff dynamics respond sharply to these parameters,
a single well-timed measurement can rule out most of that broad prior at once, and so carry
many nats of information. These two properties together are what break the contrastive
estimators on which modern BED depends. Those estimators approximate the intractable marginal
by averaging the likelihood over parameter samples drawn from the prior, so when the prior is
broad and the observation is highly informative, almost every prior sample is incompatible with
the data and contributes nothing to the average, and the information estimate stalls. We show
that any such estimator built from $L$ prior samples is capped at $\log(L+1)$ nats, and that the
number of samples needed to clear the cap grows exponentially with the information per
experiment. This is a sharp instance of a general limitation of sample-based
mutual-information estimation: any distribution-free lower bound on the information computed
from $L$ samples is itself capped at order $\log L$~\citep{mcallester2020formal}, while
estimators that are not so capped instead incur variance that grows exponentially with the true
information~\citep{song2020understanding}. Either way, no feasible budget recovers a usable
design signal on these systems.

These three properties are not independent difficulties that one might hope to meet one at a
time. They are coupled and they co-occur. The same separation of timescales that makes a network
stiff is what spreads its rate constants over orders of magnitude, which is what makes the prior
broad; and a broad prior over sharply responsive dynamics is what lets a single experiment carry
many nats of information. On the small problems that design methods are usually benchmarked on,
low-dimensional pharmacokinetic compartments, source localisation, and small ODEs, one or more
of these properties is mild or absent, which is why existing methods do well there. On the
systems that motivate this work, and on genuinely interesting dynamical systems more generally,
all three hold at once. They are the normal condition of the problem rather than a rare or
avoidable case, as the standard benchmark collections of biochemical ODE models
attest~\citep{hass2019benchmark,villaverde2016structural}. It is in exactly this regime that the
methods dominating modern sequential BED break down, and this thesis explains why and develops a
method that does not.

\section{The amortized-policy paradigm and its limits}
\label{sec:intro-obstacle}

A major thread of modern sequential BED, set out in the review of \citet{rainforth2024modern}, is
the amortized design policy: rather than re-optimising each design online against a freshly fitted
posterior, train a design policy offline and deploy it as a cheap forward pass. The review presents
this as the resolution of two obstacles of the traditional per-step approach, its expensive
per-step inference and optimisation during the experiment and its restriction to greedy, myopic
choices. Deep Adaptive Design (DAD) introduced the construction~\citep{foster2021deep}, with
extensions to likelihood-free simulators~\citep{ivanova2021implicit}, deployment-time
refinement~\citep{hedman2025stepdad}, and reinforcement-learning variants such as RL-BOED and
variational sequential design~\citep{blau2022optimizing,shen2025vsoed}; these differ in how the
policy is trained but share a contrastive, sPCE-style objective. The other approach is per-trajectory design, which
optimises each design online against the current posterior instead of learning a reusable policy,
and which has generally been found to underperform the trained
policy~\citep{ivanova2021implicit,zaballa2025bridging}.

This thesis shows that the advantage of amortized policies does not hold on the stiff,
broad-prior, high-information systems described above, for two reasons. The first concerns the
policy's training signal. Policy methods are trained against the sequential prior-contrastive
estimation (sPCE) bound~\citep{foster2019variational,foster2020unified,foster2021deep}, the
prior-averaged contrastive estimator introduced above applied to the whole trajectory, which
inherits the same $\log(L+1)$ ceiling~\citep{mcallester2020formal,paninski2005asymptotic}. When one
experiment is more informative than this ceiling, the bound pegs at it for every candidate design
and, as we show, its gradient vanishes with it, so the training signal cannot say which design is
better, while the budget needed to escape grows exponentially with the information per experiment.
We call this \emph{contrastive saturation}; it is a property of the objective, not of any network
or optimiser, so every method trained on it inherits the failure, including the
reinforcement-learning variants.

The second reason is that the premise that per-trajectory design is inherently weaker than a
trained policy does not hold. \citet{klein2026supercharging} showed that a multiple-restart gradient ascent
through a \emph{differentiable} simulator lets an online per-trajectory method match or exceed the
policy, though the demonstration is narrow: it uses hundreds of optimisation restarts on smooth,
low-dimensional benchmarks, and its own results show the advantage concentrated in the lowest
dimension and shrinking as the dimension grows. It also needs gradients through the simulator, and
on stiff ODE systems those gradients are expensive, numerically delicate, or
unavailable~\citep{kim2024stiff,pal2023neural}. The promise of per-trajectory design is real, but
so far it holds only on small, differentiable problems and at high cost.

The gap is a per-trajectory method that needs neither a trained policy nor simulator gradients: it
uses the current posterior but scores candidate designs by forward simulation alone. Such a method
would beat the amortized policy with far less computation than the gradient-based route, and on the
stiff, saturated systems where policy training is infeasible it would be the only feasible choice,
reversing the relationship found on the standard benchmarks.

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:intro-contributions}

This thesis closes that gap with a method in two independent parts. The first is \textbf{PACE}
(Posterior-Amortized Contrastive Estimation), a posterior-contrastive estimator of the per-step
information gain. It is an importance-weighted contrastive estimator, like the learned-proposal
sequential Adaptive Contrastive Estimation (sACE) bound stated but never evaluated in \citet{foster2021deep}, but it reweights toward a different
target. sACE bounds the information in the \emph{whole trajectory}, and its contrastive weights
make the denominator a prior marginal, as in the prior-contrastive sPCE; PACE targets the
\emph{per-step} information gain, and its self-normalized weights make the denominator the current
posterior-predictive. The proposal is a neural posterior estimator trained once, offline, by
simulation-based inference~\citep{cranmer2020frontier,greenberg2019automatic,durkan2019neural}:
particles are drawn from it and corrected to the true posterior by those weights. Because they
come from the posterior, these contrastives lie where the data are plausible, so they estimate the
denominator well and the score tracks the true gain instead of pinning at the $\log(L+1)$ ceiling,
at a cost we quantify below. The second part is a forward-only design optimiser: at each step it
samples candidate designs, scores each one with PACE from a single shared particle set, and refines
the search by a cross-entropy update, using forward simulations of the model and no gradient through
it. The two parts are independent: PACE removes saturation for any optimiser, and the forward-only
search is what lets the method run on the stiff, non-differentiable simulators where gradient-based
design fails. Figure~\ref{fig:hero} summarises the idea. The contribution has four components:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.25em]
  \item \textbf{A diagnosis of contrastive saturation.} We show that the sPCE bound
  equals $\log(L+1)-\log(1+\sum_\ell r_\ell)$, where $r_\ell$ is the likelihood ratio of
  contrastive $\ell$ to the data-generating parameter, that its gradient vanishes whenever
  the bound does, and that the budget needed to escape the ceiling scales as $e^{\Lambda}$,
  with $\Lambda$ the Gaussian-linearised information gain of the experiment. We give three
  mechanisms, broad priors, high-dynamic-range dynamics, and non-Gaussian noise, that make
  the failure severe on biochemical systems, and we measure escape thresholds ranging from
  below $10$ to above $10^6$ across a twelve-system suite.

  \item \textbf{The PACE estimator and the cost transfer.} We define the estimator and show it is
  consistent. The cost, however, does not vanish: as the posterior concentrates, an imperfect
  proposal diverges from it, and the effective sample size of the weights falls with the chi-square
  divergence between the two. The hardness that saturated the policy objective is therefore not
  removed but moved, from the contrastive budget into the quality of the proposal.

  \item \textbf{Validity of design selection under weight collapse.} As the effective
  sample size approaches one, PACE reduces to a weighted sum of the same likelihood ratios.
  We show that the diverging weight scale enters only terms that are equal across candidate
  designs and therefore cancels, so the ranking of designs survives, and that in this limit
  the criterion approaches a Fisher-information rule, which is $\Lambda$ in a new role.

  \item \textbf{Empirical validation on twelve systems.} PACE matches or beats the
  amortized policy on every system where the policy training signal has saturated,
  including a stiff wastewater model on which policy training is infeasible, while learned
  policies remain stronger on low-information, high-dimensional designs. This is the central
  empirical message: a posterior, per-trajectory method does not merely keep pace with
  amortized policies but, on the stiff high-information systems that motivate the work, is
  the only feasible family.
\end{enumerate}



\section{Research questions}
\label{sec:intro-rqs}

The thesis is organised around one main question: \textit{Can sequential Bayesian experimental design be made feasible and effective on the stiff, broad-prior, high-information dynamical systems where the amortized policy
objective saturates?}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item \textbf{RQ1.} When and why does the prior-contrastive sPCE objective, the training signal
  of amortized policy methods, fail on these systems, and can that failure be predicted before any
  policy is trained?
  \item \textbf{RQ2.} Can contrastives drawn from a one-time, simulation-based posterior estimator
  remove that saturation, and what new cost does the replacement introduce?
  \item \textbf{RQ3.} When the importance weights collapse onto few particles, do the resulting
  design rankings remain valid?
  \item \textbf{RQ4.} Across a broad suite of systems, when does the forward-only
  posterior-contrastive method match or exceed amortized policies, and what predicts the crossover?
\end{itemize}

\section{Thesis outline}
\label{sec:intro-outline}

Chapter~\ref{sec:related_work} positions PACE against the literature it draws on and departs from.
It develops the three lines the method touches, simulation-based inference, the contrastive-EIG
estimators that policy methods train on, and the per-trajectory and reinforcement-learning
families, so that every object the method later uses, including the learned-proposal contrastive
bounds, is introduced before it is needed, and it states the gap PACE fills.
Chapter~\ref{sec:methodology} develops the method in full. It sets up sequential BED and derives
the trajectory objective that policy methods optimise together with the contrastive estimators
used for evaluation, then presents the saturation diagnosis (RQ1), the PACE estimator and its cost
transfer (RQ2), the validity of design selection under weight collapse (RQ3), the forward-only
optimiser and its gradient variant, and the evaluation protocol. Chapter~\ref{sec:results} reports
the empirical study on twelve systems (RQ4), including the ablation that separates the estimator
from the optimiser and the diagnostics that justify trusting the method when the effective sample
size is small. Chapter~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses when PACE should be used, its practical cost,
and its limitations, and Chapter~\ref{sec:conclusion} answers each research question. Full
derivations, system specifications, and additional diagnostics are in the appendix.
