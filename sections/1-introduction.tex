\chapter{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Many experiments are defined not only by \emph{what} is done but by \emph{when} it is
done. In a biochemical reaction network, adding the same reagent early or late can lead
to different products; in a bioreactor, the same total feed can give a high or a low
yield depending on its timing; in an epidemic, the same intervention has different value
early or late in an outbreak. The same pattern appears across wastewater treatment,
cell-free biosynthesis, dynamic metabolism, bioprocess control, and battery
cycling, where the outcome depends on the schedule of inputs and not only on their
totals~\citep{henze2000activated,silverman2020cellfree,martin2023dynamic,espinelrios2024cybergenetics,vanvlijmen2025battery}.
Recent experimental work makes the point concrete. Timed additions to an enzymatic
reaction cascade raise yields well above conventional all-at-once
dosing~\citep{jakstaite2026timedbatch}, and actively designed perturbation sequences can
steer intricate reaction networks that fixed protocols cannot~\citep{vansluijs2024iterative}.
In these settings an experiment is not a single condition but a schedule: a sequence of
interventions and measurements chosen over time.

Choosing such a schedule well is hard for two reasons. The experiments are expensive, in
time, material, or effort, so only a few can be run. And the space of schedules is large,
because at each step the experimenter may choose both the size and the timing of the next
action in light of everything seen so far. Selecting inputs one step at a time, using the
outcomes so far to decide the next move, turns experimental design into a sequential
control problem over a growing, history-dependent space. Intuition and fixed protocols
are weak tools here, and exhaustive search is impossible, so the choice has to be made by
an algorithm that reasons about what each candidate experiment is expected to teach.

Sequential Bayesian experimental design (BED) is the established framework for making
that choice~\citep{lindley1956measure,chaloner1995bayesian,ryan2016review,rainforth2024modern}.
It places a prior over the unknown parameters of the system and, at each step, selects
the experiment expected to be most informative, where informativeness is measured by the
expected information gain: the expected reduction in posterior uncertainty about the
parameters after the experiment is run. Running this rule forward produces an adaptive
sequence of experiments, each chosen in light of the last, which is exactly the regime
the timed-input problems above require.

The systems where timing matters most are also the hardest for design algorithms, and the
difficulty has a specific and recurring shape. They are modelled by stiff ordinary
differential equations (ODEs), so the numerical solution is sensitive and the solver is
expensive. Their kinetic parameters are known only to within several orders of magnitude,
so the prior is broad. And because the dynamics respond strongly to those parameters, a
single well-placed measurement can rule out most of the prior and so carry many nats of
information. Each property is individually manageable. Their combination, a broad prior
and high information per experiment and a stiff simulator, is exactly where the modern
methods for sequential BED break down. This thesis explains why they break down, and
develops a method that does not.

\section{The state of the art and where it breaks}
\label{sec:intro-obstacle}

Two families of methods dominate modern sequential BED, and they take opposite stances on
where to spend computation. \emph{Policy-based} methods, beginning with Deep Adaptive
Design (DAD), train a design network offline against a large set of simulated
trajectories, so that at deployment each next design is produced by a single fast forward
pass of the network~\citep{foster2021deep,ivanova2021implicit,hedman2025stepdad}.
\emph{Per-trajectory} methods skip the offline policy and instead re-optimise the next
design online, scoring candidate designs against a freshly fitted estimate of the current
posterior~\citep{klein2026supercharging}. Both perform well on the standard benchmark
suite of pharmacokinetic compartments, source localisation, and small ODEs, and both lean
on the same enabling tool: simulation-based inference (SBI), which performs Bayesian
inference using only samples from the simulator when the likelihood cannot be evaluated in
closed form~\citep{cranmer2020frontier,greenberg2019automatic,durkan2019neural}.

Policy methods are trained against the sequential prior-contrastive estimation (sPCE)
bound on the trajectory information~\citep{foster2019variational,foster2020unified,foster2021deep}.
This bound estimates an intractable marginal likelihood by an average over $L$ parameter
samples drawn from the prior, and any contrastive lower bound built from $L$ such samples
cannot exceed $\log(L+1)$ nats~\citep{mcallester2020formal,paninski2005asymptotic}. When a
single experiment is more informative than this ceiling, almost every prior sample is
incompatible with the observed trajectory, the bound sits at its ceiling for every
candidate design, and, as we show, its gradient vanishes at the same rate. The training
signal then carries no information about which design is better. We show that the number
of contrastives needed to clear the ceiling grows exponentially with the information per
experiment, so on stiff high-information systems no feasible contrastive budget recovers a
usable signal. We call this failure \emph{contrastive saturation}. It is a property of the
objective, not of any particular network or optimiser, so every method trained on this
objective inherits it, including the reinforcement-learning variants that optimise the
same reward without differentiating through the simulator.

Per-trajectory methods avoid saturation by working with the current posterior rather than
the prior, which keeps the contrastive samples in the region that matters. But the leading
methods optimise the next design with gradients taken through the simulator, and on stiff
ODE solvers this requires an adjoint pass that is expensive, numerically delicate, or
unavailable~\citep{kim2024stiff,pal2023neural}. Neither family is therefore suited to the
stiff, broad-prior, high-information systems that motivate this work: one loses its
training signal, the other needs gradients the simulator cannot supply reliably. The gap
is a method that uses the current posterior, like the per-trajectory family, but needs
neither a trained policy nor simulator gradients, and that remains usable when the
posterior is so concentrated that its importance weights collapse.

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:intro-contributions}

This thesis introduces \textbf{PACE} (Posterior-Amortized Contrastive Estimation), a
forward-only sequential design method that escapes contrastive saturation without
differentiating through the simulator. PACE draws its contrastive samples from an
amortized neural posterior estimator, trained once by simulation-based
inference~\citep{cranmer2020frontier,greenberg2019automatic,durkan2019neural}, and
corrects them with self-normalized importance weights. It then selects designs by forward
search rather than by a trained policy or by simulator gradients.
Figure~\ref{fig:hero} summarises the idea.

A single mathematical object runs through the whole argument. It is the likelihood ratio
$r_\ell$ between a contrastive parameter and the data-generating one, whose prior average
is $e^{-\Lambda}$, where $\Lambda$ is the Gaussian-linearised information gain of the
experiment. This one quantity drives saturation, governs the failure of the proposed fix,
becomes the design criterion when that failure is most severe, and predicts when the
method wins. The four contributions trace it through the method.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.25em]
  \item \textbf{A diagnosis of contrastive saturation.} We show that the sPCE bound
  equals $\log(L+1)-\log(1+\sum_\ell r_\ell)$, that its gradient vanishes whenever the
  bound does, and that the escape budget scales as $e^{\Lambda}$. We give three
  mechanisms, broad priors, high-dynamic-range dynamics, and non-Gaussian noise, that make
  the failure severe on biochemical systems, and we measure escape thresholds ranging from
  below $10$ to above $10^6$ across a twelve-system suite.

  \item \textbf{The PACE estimator and a cost transfer.} Replacing prior contrastives with
  posterior particles removes saturation: the likelihood factor in the importance weight
  is exactly what cancels the divergence that defeated prior contrastives. The cost does
  not disappear. As the posterior contracts, the importance weights lose effective sample
  size, and the same concentration that caused saturation reappears, moved from policy
  training to amortized inference.

  \item \textbf{Validity of design selection under weight collapse.} As the effective
  sample size approaches one, PACE reduces to a weighted sum of the same likelihood ratios.
  We show that the diverging weight scale enters only terms that are equal across candidate
  designs and therefore cancels, so the ranking of designs survives, and that in this limit
  the criterion approaches a Fisher-information rule, which is $\Lambda$ in a new role.

  \item \textbf{Empirical validation on twelve systems.} PACE matches or beats the
  amortized policy on every system where the policy training signal has saturated,
  including a stiff wastewater model on which policy training is infeasible, while learned
  policies remain stronger on low-information, high-dimensional designs. This is the central
  empirical message: a posterior, per-trajectory method does not merely keep pace with
  amortized policies but, on the stiff high-information systems that motivate the work, is
  the only feasible family. The crossover is predicted by the saturation threshold, not by
  the effective sample size.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Research questions}
\label{sec:intro-rqs}

The thesis is organised around one main question. It asks whether the obstacle above can
be removed without trading it for a worse one, and the four sub-questions follow the path
of the single quantity from the diagnosis of the failure, through the fix and its new
cost, to the regime where the method is needed.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Main question: Can sequential Bayesian experimental design be made
feasible and effective on stiff, broad-prior dynamical systems where amortized policy
methods fail?}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item \textbf{RQ1.} When and why does the prior-contrastive sPCE objective, the training
  signal shared by amortized policy methods, fail, and can the failure be quantified before
  any policy is trained?
  \item \textbf{RQ2.} Can a one-time simulation-based posterior estimator supply
  contrastives that escape saturation, and what new cost does this introduce?
  \item \textbf{RQ3.} Does design selection remain valid when the importance weights
  collapse to a single particle?
  \item \textbf{RQ4.} Across a broad suite of systems, when does forward-only
  posterior-contrastive design match or beat amortized policies, and what predicts the
  crossover?
\end{itemize}

\section{Thesis outline}
\label{sec:intro-outline}

Chapter~\ref{sec:related_work} positions PACE against the literature it draws on and
departs from. It develops the three lines the method touches, simulation-based inference,
the contrastive-EIG estimators that policy methods train on, and the per-trajectory and
reinforcement-learning families, in enough detail that the reader meets every object PACE
uses, including the learned-proposal contrastive bounds, before the method is derived, and
it states the gap PACE fills. Chapter~\ref{sec:methodology} develops the method in full:
it sets up sequential BED, derives the trajectory objective that policy methods optimise
and the contrastive estimators used for evaluation, then presents the saturation diagnosis
(RQ1), the PACE estimator and its cost transfer (RQ2), the analysis of design selection
under weight collapse (RQ3), the forward-only optimiser and its gradient variant, and the
evaluation protocol. Chapter~\ref{sec:results} reports the empirical study on twelve
systems (RQ4), including the ablation that separates the estimator from the optimiser and
the diagnostics that justify trusting the method when the effective sample size is small.
Chapter~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses when PACE should be used, its practical cost, and
its limitations, and Chapter~\ref{sec:conclusion} answers each research question. Full
derivations, system specifications, and additional diagnostics are in the appendix; the
main text is self-contained without it.
